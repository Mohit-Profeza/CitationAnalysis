<h2 id="atl">Dietary fat overcomes the protective activity of thrombospondin-1 signaling in the <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> model of colon cancer<br /><span class="open-access">Open</span></h2>
<p id="aug">D R&nbsp;Soto-Pantoja<sup><a title="affiliated with 1" href="#aff1">1</a>,<a title="affiliated with 2" href="#aff2">2</a></sup>, J M&nbsp;Sipes<sup><a title="affiliated with 3" href="#aff3">3</a></sup>, G&nbsp;Martin-Manso<sup><a title="affiliated with 3" href="#aff3">3</a></sup>, B&nbsp;Westwood<sup><a title="affiliated with 2" href="#aff2">2</a></sup>, N L&nbsp;Morris<sup><a title="affiliated with 4" href="#aff4">4</a></sup>, A&nbsp;Ghosh<sup><a title="affiliated with 3" href="#aff3">3</a></sup>, N J&nbsp;Emenaker<sup><a title="affiliated with 5" href="#aff5">5</a></sup> and D D&nbsp;Roberts<sup><a title="affiliated with 3" href="#aff3">3</a></sup></p><div id="affiliations-notes"><ol><li id="aff1"><sup>1</sup>Department of Cancer Biology, Comprehensive Cancer Center, Wake Forest School of Medicine, Winston-Salem, NC, USA</li><li id="aff2"><sup>2</sup>Department of Surgery, Comprehensive Cancer Center, Wake Forest School of Medicine, Winston-Salem, NC, USA</li><li id="aff3"><sup>3</sup>Laboratory of Pathology, Center for Cancer Research, National Cancer Institute, NIH, Bethesda, MD, USA</li><li id="aff4"><sup>4</sup>Chemical Biology Laboratory, National Laboratory for Cancer Research, Leidos Biomedical Research, Frederick, MD, USA</li><li id="aff5"><sup>5</sup>Nutritional Science Research Group, Division of Cancer Prevention, National Cancer Institute, NIH, Bethesda, MD, USA</li></ol><p class="caff">Correspondence: Dr DR Soto-Pantoja, Department of Cancer Biology, Comprehensive Cancer Center, Wake Forest School of Medicine, Medical Center Boulevard, Winston-Salem, NC 27157, USA E-mail: <a href="mailto:dsotopan@wakehealth.edu">dsotopan@wakehealth.edu</a>; Dr DD Roberts, Laboratory of Pathology, Center for Cancer Research, National Cancer Institute, NIH, Building 10 Room 2A33, 10 Center Drive MSC1500, Bethesda, MD 20892-1500, USA. E-mail: <a href="mailto:droberts@helix.nih.gov">droberts@helix.nih.gov</a></p><p class="prdates">Received 23&nbsp;December&nbsp;2015; Revised 14&nbsp;April&nbsp;2016; Accepted 16&nbsp;April&nbsp;2016</p></div>
<div id="abs"><a class="backtotop" href="#top">Top<span class="hidden"> of page</span></a><h3>Abstract</h3><p class="abs lead">Thrombospondin 1 is a glycoprotein that regulates cellular phenotype through interactions with its cellular receptors and extracellular matrix-binding partners. Thrombospondin 1 locally regulates angiogenesis and inflammatory responses that contribute to colorectal carcinogenesis in <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> mice. The ability of thrombospondin 1 to regulate responses of cells and tissues to a variety of stresses suggested that loss of thrombospondin 1 may also have broader systemic effects on metabolism to modulate carcinogenesis. <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> mice exhibited decreased survival and higher tumor multiplicities in the small and large intestine relative to <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> mice when fed a low (5<span class="mb">&#x00025;</span>) fat western diet. However, the protective effect of endogenous thrombospondin 1 was lost when the mice were fed a western diet containing 21<span class="mb">&#x00025;</span> fat. Biochemical profiles of liver tissue identified systemic metabolic changes accompanying the effects of thrombospondin 1 and dietary lipid intake on tumorigenesis. A high-fat western diet differentially regulated elements of amino acid, energy and lipid metabolism in <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup>:<i>Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> mice relative to <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup>mice. Metabolic changes in ketone body and tricarboxylic acid cycle intermediates indicate functional interactions between Apc and thrombospondin 1 signaling that control mitochondrial function. The cumulative diet-dependent differential changes observed in <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> versus <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> mice include altered amino acid and lipid metabolism, mitochondrial dysfunction, eicosanoids and ketone body formation. This metabolic profile suggests that the protective role of thrombospondin 1 to decrease adenoma formation in <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> mice results in part from improved mitochondrial function.</p></div>
<!-- articlebody start --><div id="articlebody"><div id="Introduction"><a class="backtotop" href="#top">Top<span class="hidden"> of page</span></a><h3>Introduction</h3><p class="norm">Colon cancer is major public health concern with over 130<span class="mb"><span class="mb">&thinsp;</span></span>000 new cases diagnosed every year and over 50<span class="mb"><span class="mb">&thinsp;</span></span>000 deaths in the United States alone.<sup><!--bib1--><a href="#bib1">1</a></sup> While many factors including diet and genetics influence colon cancer progression,<sup><!--bib2--><a href="#bib2">2</a></sup> expression of thrombospondin-1 (TSP1) inversely correlates with colon cancer aggressiveness.<sup><!--bib3--><a href="#bib3">3</a>, <!--bib4--><a href="#bib4">4</a></sup> TSP1 is a matricellular protein that regulates tissue perfusion, platelet aggregation, angiogenesis, and responses to stress.<sup><!--bib4--><a href="#bib4">4</a>, <!--bib5--><a href="#bib5">5</a>, <!--bib6--><a href="#bib6">6</a></sup> Spontaneous tumors can be demonstrated in TSP1 null mice only when they are crossed with other strains that are cancer prone such as the MMTV-Neu model or <i>p53</i> null mice.<sup><!--bib7--><a href="#bib7">7</a>, <!--bib8--><a href="#bib8">8</a></sup> In several such carcinogenesis models TSP1 expression has been demonstrated to delay premalignant hyperplasia, tumorigenesis, tumor angiogenesis and<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>or metastasis.<sup><!--bib7--><a href="#bib7">7</a>, <!--bib8--><a href="#bib8">8</a>, <!--bib9--><a href="#bib9">9</a>, <!--bib10--><a href="#bib10">10</a>, <!--bib11--><a href="#bib11">11</a></sup></p><p class="norm">Over 5<span class="mb">&#x00025;</span> of colorectal cancer cases are due to a genetic predisposition, and one frequent abnormality causing predisposition to human colorectal cancer is mutation in the adenomatous polyposis coli (<i>APC</i>) gene.<sup><!--bib12--><a href="#bib12">12</a></sup> In humans, familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP) is characterized by the onset of multiple adenomas in the colon that can progress to tumors and metastatic forms of colorectal cancer. In mice, heterozygous mutation (<i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup>) has generated a clinically relevant model to study colorectal cancer that has provided insights into the onset of sporadic colorectal cancer.<sup><!--bib12--><a href="#bib12">12</a>, <!--bib13--><a href="#bib13">13</a></sup> These mice develop many of the characteristics of human FAP patients but have some limitations including aggressivenes of the disease and the formation of most adenomas in the small intestine.<sup><!--bib14--><a href="#bib14">14</a></sup> Still many translational applications have been tested successfully in this model including the use NSAID&#x02019;s to prevent or treat colorectal cancer.<sup><!--bib12--><a href="#bib12">12</a></sup> The <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup>mouse correspondingly is a widely used model for studying these cancers. To monitor the spontaneous colorectal tumor growth, and the survival of mice exposed to a low fat or western diet, we generated a double transgenic animal harboring the <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> locus and lacking TSP1 (<i>Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup>). Previous studies in the <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> murine model of colon cancer demonstrated that loss of TSP1 increases tumor multiplicity in the small and large intestines.<sup><!--bib15--><a href="#bib15">15</a></sup> The absence of TSP1 in this model was correlated with an increase in TUNEL positive nuclei in the polyps lacking TSP1. A majority of these involved amino acid, eicosanoid and lipid metabolism. Ketogenic amino acids and lipid metabolism changes were associated with dysregulation of TCA cycle metabolites that suggest mitochondrial dysfunction in livers of the <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> mice. The dysregulation of cell energy metabolites was associated with increased tumor multiplicity and decreased survival of <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> mice when fed a low-fat diet. The increase in tumor multiplicity was observed in both the small and large intestine.</p><p class="norm">The <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> model is a clinically relevant tumor model of FAP and spontaneous colon cancer. However one of the limitations of this model is that most lesions are observed in the small intestines, which does not mirror the human disease. Deletion of TSP1 caused an increased in colonic lesions independent of diet when compared with <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup>, which may be a more clinically relevant model to study colon cancer as it mimics the observed loss of TSP1 and the phenotype associated with it in humans. Other genetic deletions in the <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> mouse have led to increased lesions. Absence of glutathione transferase gene in the <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> mouse led to six-fold increase in colon lesions.<sup><!--bib48--><a href="#bib48">48</a></sup> This was attributed in part to increase in pro-inflammatory signaling and regulation of iNOS. The <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> mice also showed regulation of inflammatory signaling and showed regulation of reduced glutathione and oxidized glutathione in livers, which could explain the increased lesions in the large intestine in both models.</p><p class="norm">Several studies have demonstrated that loss or inhibition of TSP1 expression increases colon carcinogenesis and malignant progression.<sup><!--bib49--><a href="#bib49">49</a>, <!--bib50--><a href="#bib50">50</a>, <!--bib51--><a href="#bib51">51</a>, <!--bib52--><a href="#bib52">52</a></sup> Decreased TSP1 expression in adenomas of <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> mice was associated with a reduced apoptotic index, increase in proliferation and differential patterns of vessel density.<sup><!--bib15--><a href="#bib15">15</a></sup> <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> showed increased vascularization in pre-malignant intestinal tissue but no significant differences in vascularization between adenomas and carcinomas between <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> and <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup>. This also correlated with no differences in expression of VEGF, which led to the conclusion that TSP1 may regulate the initial stages of carcinogenesis when neovascularization to sustain a tumor lesion is low. Correspondingly, once tumors became well established, the effect of TSP1 deletion was decreased . However, our studies indicate that TSP1 may also be involved in later stages of colon carcinogenesis because <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> animals that were fed a low-fat diet, which can decrease colon cancer formation, showed decreased survival in the absence of TSP1 expression. In the context of a high-fat diet, the absence of TSP1 led to multiple metabolic alterations in livers of 12-week-old mice that could explain late-stage systemic effects of TSP1 on colon cancer progression.</p><p class="norm">Our study indicates that an increase in TCA cycle metabolites could be due to the increased glycolytic flux, which is characteristic of colon cancer.<sup><!--bib53--><a href="#bib53">53</a></sup> Consistent with this hypothesis, loss of the TSP1 receptor CD47 altered both glycolytic and TCA cycle flux in a human T-cell line and <i>cd47</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> mice.<sup><!--bib21--><a href="#bib21">21</a></sup> However, the present results suggest decreased TCA cycle activity and oxidative phosphorylation. As both &#x003B2;-oxidation and ketogenesis occur in the mitochondria, and medium-chain free fatty acids can freely cross mitochondrial membranes to facilitate &#x003B2;-oxidation, one explanation for the cumulative differential changes observed in <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> mice fed a high-fat diet may involve mitochondrial dysfunction, resulting in decreased fatty acid oxidation and ketone body formation, along with the accumulation of free fatty acids. Perturbations in the TCA cycle intermediates and biochemicals related to lipid metabolism are indicative of changes in fatty acid handling and utilization in high-fat diet-fed <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> mice as compared with the other study groups. Cancer cells use amino acids as energy sources or to generate metabolites to fuel cell proliferation and growth.<sup><!--bib54--><a href="#bib54">54</a></sup> We observed that amino-acid metabolite levels were increased in <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> fed a low-fat diet when compared with <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> alone. This could contribute to the differences in tumor multiplicity and survival within this group, as other studies where serum metabolites were analyzed found increased amino-acid metabolites in <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> mice as well as in the serum of mice bearing colon tumors of the HT-29 cell line where the <i>APC</i> gene was truncated.<sup><!--bib28--><a href="#bib28">28</a></sup> On the other hand, some amino-acid metabolite levels were depleted in the <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> fed a high-fat diet when compared with <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> mice. This is a less surprising finding as this could indicate that the low levels of amino-acid metabolites are due in part to increased uptake to fuel cell growth. Lower liver levels of ketogenic amino acids and metabolites may reflect higher mitochondrial &#x003B2;-oxidation in mice fed the high-fat diet. Consistent with this hypothesis, amino-acid levels were depleted by high fat intake in drosophila.<sup><!--bib55--><a href="#bib55">55</a></sup> This was attributed to increased levels of urea and uric acid affecting nitrogen metabolism and the increased analplerotic substrates in the TCA cycle. In circumstances where fat is the main source of energy, lipids cannot readily enter the TCA cycle via anaplerotic pathways to generate acetyl-CoA.<sup><!--bib55--><a href="#bib55">55</a></sup> In this situation, levels of acetyl-CoA are generated via &#x003B2;-oxidation. Therefore, amino-acid metabolism can serve as a primary source of anaplerotic substrates to sustain the TCA cycle and become depleted during instances of low glucose utilization.<sup><!--bib55--><a href="#bib55">55</a></sup> Another point of regulation of the TCA cycle in our models is indicated by the levels of 3-hydroxybutyrate in <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> mice. 3-Hydroxybutyrate is generated in the liver from acetyl-CoA during periods of low glucose levels or starvation. This should occur in the context of a low-fat diet that is not deficient in carbohydrate sources, thus indicating a shift in glucose utilization to ketone body formation as a source of energy may be driven by the absence of TSP1 in the <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> mice. During starvation, ketone bodies are used as an energy source, and the decreased use of glucose by peripheral organs such as the liver suggest an increased dependence on fat as an energy source.<sup><!--bib56--><a href="#bib56">56</a></sup> Since <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> have increased tumor multiplicity, glucose utilization may shift toward tumors while leaving peripheral organs to rely on fatty acid and ketone body formation to maintain energy levels. This shift in ketone body and glucose utilization due to starvation can increase pathways such as autophagy that cause increased carcinogenesis. Autophagy is demonstrated to be activated in murine intestinal epithelium and human colon cancer.<sup><!--bib57--><a href="#bib57">57</a>, <!--bib58--><a href="#bib58">58</a></sup> In an <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> model, deletion of autophagy-related protein-7 (<i>Atg7</i>) led to AMPK activation and reduction in carcinogenesis.<sup><!--bib57--><a href="#bib57">57</a></sup> Loss of the TSP1<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>CD47 signaling axis is implicated in the activation autophagy in non-transformed tissue.<sup><!--bib59--><a href="#bib59">59</a></sup> Although the role of TSP1<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>CD47 signaling in regulation of autophagy in cancer is unknown, circulating levels of TSP1 may inhibit this pathway to ameliorate cancer development.</p><p class="norm">Moreover, <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> <i>mice</i> fed a low-fat diet show elevated levels of 2HG, which is known as an oncometabolite.<sup><!--bib60--><a href="#bib60">60</a></sup> Several factors including mutations in isocitrate dehydrogenase lead to accumulation of 2HG, which is considered a poor prognostic factor in many cancers including colon cancer.<sup><!--bib61--><a href="#bib61">61</a>, <!--bib62--><a href="#bib62">62</a></sup> The accumulation of this metabolite is another indication of defects in metabolism that are caused by the deletion of <i>Thbs1</i> in the <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> mice and could lead to more aggressive formation of tumors in this model.</p><p class="norm">The negative effects of endogenous TSP1 on PGE2 levels in mice fed the low-fat diet may also contribute to the delay of carcinogenesis by endogenous TSP1. The loss of PGE2 suppression by TSP1 in mice fed the high-fat diet is consistent with our finding that the high-fat diet suppressed the protective effects of TSP1 in <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> mice. COX2 expression is elevated in polyps of <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> mice<sup><!--bib34--><a href="#bib34">34</a></sup> and in human colorectal cancer.<sup><!--bib63--><a href="#bib63">63</a></sup> Apc<sup>Min</sup> is defective in regulation of COX2 via inhibition of CtBP1.<sup><!--bib36--><a href="#bib36">36</a></sup> COX2 inhibitors are well documented by epidemiological studies to delay colon carcinogenesis.<sup><!--bib64--><a href="#bib64">64</a>, <!--bib65--><a href="#bib65">65</a></sup> Thus, our data indicates that TSP1, Apc and dietary fat are convergent regulators of PGE2 levels. <i>Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> mice have elevated liver levels of multiple eicosanoid metabolites including 9-HODE, 13-HODE and 12,13-diHOME, which suggest that the regulation of PGE2 levels by TSP1 occurs upstream at least at the level of linoleate availability. The proximal molecular targets of TSP1 signaling in the eicosanoid pathway remain to be determined.</p><p class="norm">Studies have demonstrated that a low-fat diet can prevent colon tumor formation, but the relative risk associated with dietary fat in humans remains unclear.<sup><!--bib66--><a href="#bib66">66</a>, <!--bib67--><a href="#bib67">67</a></sup> Our study demonstrates a protective effect of endogenous TSP1 that is sensitive to dietary fat intake. The loss of TSP1 expression that has been documented to result from loss of tumor suppresser genes and activation of oncogenes associated with colon carcinogenesis could contribute to promoting early and late stages of colon tumor formation.<sup><!--bib4--><a href="#bib4">4</a></sup> Thus, <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> mice could be useful to identify TSP1-independent effects of cancer genes on cancer progression. Another advantage of the <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> model is that more tumors form in the large intestine when compared with the <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> mice. Therefore, this model may be more clinically relevant to study the role of APC in human colon carcinogenesis and responses to treatment.</p></div><div id="Materials-and-methods"><a class="backtotop" href="#top">Top<span class="hidden"> of page</span></a><h3>Materials and methods</h3><h4 class="norm">Apc<sup>Min</sup> model of colorectal cancer</h4><p class="follows-h4">Experimental protocols, housing and care of mice were conducted in an AAALAC-approved facility according to the animal study protocols LP-026, approved by the National Cancer Institute Animal Care and Use Committee, and ASP 12-461 approved by the NCI-Frederick Animal Care and Use Committee. WT, <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> mice and <i>Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> mice on a C57Bl<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>6 background were purchased from Jackson Laboratories. All mice were maintained and bred at least two generations on a low fat western diet (AIN-76A containing 5<span class="mb">&#x00025;</span>. at, Research Diets) to equalize dietary effects on their epigenetic context and microbiomes. <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> mice were crossed with <i>Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> mice to generate the <i>Thbs1<span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>-</i>:<i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup> strain, and these mice were crossed with <i>Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup> mice to generate <i>Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup>:<i>Apc<sup>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span></sup></i> mice. Mice were genotyped prior to weaning and randomized by stratification keeping equal sample sizes exposed to diets during the same period of time.</p><p class="norm">The <i>Apc</i> locus was genotyped using allele-specific SNP PCR. Briefly mouse tail genomic DNA was extracted using Puregene Core Kit A (part number 1042601). Twenty nanograms of DNA was added to 1 &#x000D7; TaqMan Genotyping Master Mix (Applied Biosystems Part number 4371355) containing the following Custom TaqMan SNP Genotyping Assay primers: forward</p><p class="norm">AHD16ZB_F 5&#x02032;-GGGAAGTTTAGACAGTTCTCGTTCT-3&#x02032; and reverse AHD16ZB_R 5&#x02032;-TAAGCACTGAGGCCAATACCT-3&#x02032; and the minor groove-binder probes AHD16ZB_V VIC dye-5&#x02032;-CTCTCTCCAAACTTCT-3&#x02032;-quencher and AHD16ZB_M FAM dye-5&#x02032;-TCTCTCTCCTAACTTCT-3&#x02032;-quencher. PCR amplification was performed using an Applied Biosystem ViiA 7 Real-Time PCR System: 60<span class="mb"><span class="mb">&thinsp;</span></span>&#x000B0;C for 30<span class="mb"><span class="mb">&thinsp;</span></span>s, 95<span class="mb"><span class="mb">&thinsp;</span></span>&#x000B0;C for 10<span class="mb"><span class="mb">&thinsp;</span></span>min followed by 40 cycles of 92<span class="mb"><span class="mb">&thinsp;</span></span>&#x000B0;C for 15<span class="mb"><span class="mb">&thinsp;</span></span>s and 60<span class="mb"><span class="mb">&thinsp;</span></span>&#x000B0;C for 1<span class="mb"><span class="mb">&thinsp;</span></span>min. The <i>Thbs1</i> locus was genotyped using PCR primers as described.<sup><!--bib68--><a href="#bib68">68</a></sup></p><p class="norm">Mice were pair-fed a low fat (AIN-76A 5<span class="mb">&#x00025;</span> fat) or a 21<span class="mb">&#x00025;</span> high-fat western diet (<span class="mb">&#x00023;</span>D12079B, Research Diets) beginning at weaning. The two diets contained equal percentages of protein by weight in the form of casein and equal contents of vitamin and mineral supplements. Basal 5<span class="mb">&#x00025;</span> fat in both diets was provided by corn oil, and the high-fat diet was supplemented to 21<span class="mb">&#x00025;</span> with anhydrous milk fat. Carbohydrates provided by cornstarch, maltodextrin and sucrose were adjusted to yield similar caloric content (3902<span class="mb"><span class="mb">&thinsp;</span></span>kcal<span class="mb">&#x00025;</span> in AIN76A and 4686<span class="mb"><span class="mb">&thinsp;</span></span>kcal<span class="mb">&#x00025;</span> in D12079B). One set of mice was killed at 12 weeks for metabolic and histologic analyses. Livers were harvested between 1300<span class="mb"><span class="mb">&thinsp;</span></span>hours and 1500<span class="mb"><span class="mb">&thinsp;</span></span>hours to control for circadian changes in metabolism and immediately frozen for metabolomics analysis. Additional groups of mice were maintained on low- or high-fat diets until they reached humane endpoints for survival analysis.</p><h4 class="norm">Liver tissue metabolomics</h4><p class="follows-h4">Livers of pair-fed, 12-week-old mice of the described genotypes were extracted, and weighted and equal amounts of liver tissue were flash frozen and submitted to Metabolon, Inc for extraction and analysis as shown previously.<sup><!--bib21--><a href="#bib21">21</a></sup> Briefly, the liquid chromatography<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>mass spectrometry portion of the platform was based on a Waters ACQUITY ultra-performance liquid chromatography and a Thermo Scientific Q-Exactive high resolution<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>accurate mass spectrometer interfaced with a heated electrospray ionization (HESI-II) source and Orbitrap mass analyzer operated at 35,000 mass resolution.</p><div class="norm"><!-- . --></div><h4 class="norm">Histology</h4><p class="follows-h4">Small and large intestines were collected from mice at 12 weeks and at the time of death. Tissues were fixed in 10<span class="mb">&#x00025;</span> buffered formalin and subsequently sent for paraffin embedding. Tissues were stained with hematoxylin and eosin to determine the structure and were examined under light microscopy. TUNEL staining was performed as described previously.<sup><!--bib59--><a href="#bib59">59</a></sup> Intestinal lesions were counted blindly by a pathologist from the Laboratory of Pathology at the National Cancer Institute in five fields at a magnification of &#x000D7; 20.</p><div class="norm"><!-- . --></div><h4 class="norm">Statistical analysis</h4><p class="follows-h4">Missing values were assumed to be below the level of detection. Biochemicals that were detected in all the samples from one or more groups, but not in samples from other groups were assumed to be near the lower limit of detection in the groups in which they were not detected. In this case, the lowest detected level of these biochemicals was imputed for samples in which that biochemical was not detected. Following log transformation and imputation with minimum observed values for each compound, data were protein normalized by Bradford protein assay, and both an ANOVA contrast and two-way ANOVA with random effects were used to identify biochemicals that differed significantly between experimental groups. An estimate of the false discovery rate (<i>q</i>-value) was calculated to take into account the multiple comparisons that normally occur in metabolomic-based studies. Application of principal component analysis was used to determine separation of study groups (<i>N</i><span class="mb">&#x0003D;</span>8 WT, TSP1 null, <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup>and <i>N</i><span class="mb">&#x0003D;</span>7 <i>Apc</i><sup><i>Min<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></i></sup><i>:Thbs1</i><sup><i>&#x02212;<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>&#x02212;</i></sup>). Pathways were assigned for each metabolite, allowing examination of overrepresented pathways. Survival of mice was measured over time and evaluated using log-rank (Mantel&#x02013;Cox test and Grehan&#x02013;Breslow&#x02013;Wilcoxon test, <i>N</i><span class="mb">&#x0003D;</span>14).</p><div class="norm"><!-- . --></div></div>
