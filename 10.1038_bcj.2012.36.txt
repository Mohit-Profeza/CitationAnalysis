<h2 id="atl">Stromal cells expressing hedgehog-interacting protein regulate the proliferation of myeloid neoplasms</h2>
<p id="aug">M&nbsp;Kobune<sup><a title="affiliated with 1" href="#aff1">1</a></sup>, S&nbsp;Iyama<sup><a title="affiliated with 1" href="#aff1">1</a></sup>, S&nbsp;Kikuchi<sup><a title="affiliated with 1" href="#aff1">1</a></sup>, H&nbsp;Horiguchi<sup><a title="affiliated with 1" href="#aff1">1</a></sup>, T&nbsp;Sato<sup><a title="affiliated with 1" href="#aff1">1</a></sup>, K&nbsp;Murase<sup><a title="affiliated with 1" href="#aff1">1</a></sup>, Y&nbsp;Kawano<sup><a title="affiliated with 1" href="#aff1">1</a></sup>, K&nbsp;Takada<sup><a title="affiliated with 1" href="#aff1">1</a></sup>, K&nbsp;Ono<sup><a title="affiliated with 1" href="#aff1">1</a></sup>, Y&nbsp;Kamihara<sup><a title="affiliated with 1" href="#aff1">1</a></sup>, T&nbsp;Hayashi<sup><a title="affiliated with 1" href="#aff1">1</a></sup>, K&nbsp;Miyanishi<sup><a title="affiliated with 1" href="#aff1">1</a></sup>, Y&nbsp;Sato<sup><a title="affiliated with 1" href="#aff1">1</a></sup>, R&nbsp;Takimoto<sup><a title="affiliated with 1" href="#aff1">1</a></sup> and J&nbsp;Kato<sup><a title="affiliated with 1" href="#aff1">1</a></sup></p><div id="affiliations-notes"><p id="aff"><a name="aff1"><sup>1</sup></a>Fourth Department of Internal Medicine, Sapporo Medical University School of Medicine, Sapporo, Japan</p><p class="caff">Correspondence: Dr J Kato, Fourth Department of Internal Medicine, Sapporo Medical University School of Medicine, South-1, West-16 Chuo-ku, Sapporo 060-8543, Japan. E-mail: <a href="mailto:jkato@sapmed.ac.jp">jkato@sapmed.ac.jp</a></p><p class="prdates">Received 1&nbsp;May&nbsp;2012; Revised 1&nbsp;August&nbsp;2012; Accepted 7&nbsp;August&nbsp;2012</p></div>
<div id="abs"><a class="backtotop" href="#top">Top<span class="hidden"> of page</span></a><h3>Abstract</h3><p class="abs lead">Aberrant reactivation of hedgehog (Hh) signaling has been described in a wide variety of human cancers including cancer stem cells. However, involvement of the Hh-signaling system in the bone marrow (BM) microenvironment during the development of myeloid neoplasms is unknown. In this study, we assessed the expression of Hh-related genes in primary human CD34<sup><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></sup> cells, CD34<sup><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></sup> blastic cells and BM stromal cells. Both Indian Hh (Ihh) and its signal transducer, smoothened (SMO), were expressed in CD34<sup><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></sup> acute myeloid leukemia (AML) and myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS)-derived cells. However, Ihh expression was relatively low in BM stromal cells. Remarkably, expression of the intrinsic Hh-signaling inhibitor, human Hh-interacting protein (HHIP) in AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS-derived stromal cells was markedly lower than in healthy donor-derived stromal cells. Moreover, HHIP expression levels in BM stromal cells highly correlated with their supporting activity for SMO<sup><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></sup> leukemic cells. Knockdown of <i>HHIP</i> gene in stromal cells increased their supporting activity although control cells marginally supported SMO<sup><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></sup> leukemic cell proliferation. The demethylating agent, 5-aza-2&#x02032;-deoxycytidine rescued HHIP expression via demethylation of <i>HHIP</i> gene and reduced the leukemic cell-supporting activity of AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS-derived stromal cells. This indicates that suppression of stromal HHIP could be associated with the proliferation of AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS cells.</p><div class="keyw-abbr"><h4 class="keywords">Keywords: </h4><p class="keywords">acute myeloid leukemia (AML); myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS); human hedgehog-interacting protein (HHIP); stromal cells</p></div></div>
<!-- articlebody start --><div id="articlebody"><div id="Introduction"><a class="backtotop" href="#top">Top<span class="hidden"> of page</span></a><h3>Introduction</h3><p class="norm">Recent massive parallel sequencing has enabled the analysis of whole genomes and transcriptomes from clinical samples derived from acute myeloid leukemia (AML) and myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS). Currently it is possible to comprehensively identify genetic mutations, including single base changes, deletions, insertions and genomic rearrangements in early events for AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS development.<sup><!--bib1--><a href="#bib1">1</a></sup> As a hypothetical model, the molecular pathogenesis of AML requires cooperating mutations of several genes such as early mutation, class-I and class-II mutations.<sup><!--bib1--><a href="#bib1">1</a></sup> Early mutations are present in clinically silent pre-leukemic cells, and involve genes for nucleolar ribonucleoprotein, nucleophosmin (NPM1) and the RNA splicing machinery.<sup><!--bib2--><a href="#bib2">2</a>, <!--bib3--><a href="#bib3">3</a></sup> Class-I and class-II mutations are mainly present in leukemic cells.<sup><!--bib1--><a href="#bib1">1</a></sup> Class-I mutations constitutively activate genes in the kinase signaling pathways, including <i>FLT3, c-KIT, c-FMS</i> and <i>RAS</i>, which enhance the proliferation of leukemic cells, whereas class-II mutations inactivate hematopoietic transcription factors such as AML1, ASXL, TET2 and IDH, which are associated with epigenetic regulation of gene expression.<sup><!--bib4--><a href="#bib4">4</a></sup> Although combined genetic abnormalities are likely essential for the development of AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS, aberrant function and chromosomal abnormality of human bone marrow (BM) stromal cells could be involved MDS development and subsequent secondary AML.<sup><!--bib5--><a href="#bib5">5</a></sup> Interestingly, impaired microRNA biogenesis in human BM stromal cells from MDS patients, with <i>Dicer1</i> and <i>Drosha</i> gene and protein downregulation, correlated with abnormal gene and microRNA expression.<sup><!--bib6--><a href="#bib6">6</a></sup> Moreover, mice genetically deficient for Dicer1 in BM stromal cells, developed dysplastic changes in hematopoietic cells, subsequent genetic mutations and eventually leukemic transformation.<sup><!--bib7--><a href="#bib7">7</a>, <!--bib8--><a href="#bib8">8</a></sup> Based on these findings, the function of BM stromal cells in AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS has gained increasing attention in hemato-oncology.</p><p class="norm">We and others have demonstrated that the hedgehog (Hh) signaling pathway is important in the regulation of stem<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>progenitor cell expansion and lymphocyte differentiation.<sup><!--bib9--><a href="#bib9">9</a>, <!--bib10--><a href="#bib10">10</a>, <!--bib11--><a href="#bib11">11</a></sup> In particular, Indian Hh (Ihh), its receptor patched (Ptc) and a constitutively active signal transducer, smoothened (SMO), are expressed in cord blood (CB) CD34<sup><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></sup> cells and BM stromal cells. Furthermore, changes in the cytokine expression profile of human stromal cells treated with Ihh ligand derived from CD34<sup><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></sup> cells through Hh receptor complex signaling induced the proliferation of hematopoietic stem<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>progenitor cells.<sup><!--bib11--><a href="#bib11">11</a></sup></p><p class="norm">Thus, Hh acts on stromal cells to regulate hematopoietic stem<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>progenitor cells. The replating capacity of the primary CD34<sup><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></sup> leukemic cells was significantly reduced following coculture with 5-aza-dC-pretreated AML st-1 or MDS st-9, compared with non-treated stromal cells. These results indicated that 5-aza-dC pretreatment restored HHIP expression and concomitantly reduced leukemic cell-supporting activity in primary AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS-derived stromal cells. Moreover, this effect was significantly negated by HHIP shRNA transfer. Thus, HHIP expression in primary stromal cells could be highly associated with leukemic cell-supporting activity.</p></div><div id="Discussion"><a class="backtotop" href="#top">Top<span class="hidden"> of page</span></a><h3>Discussion</h3><p class="norm">In the present study, we demonstrated that stromal HHIP expression could suppress leukemic cell proliferation. HHIP expression in AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS-derived BM stromal cells was significantly reduced, and these low HHIP-expressing stromal cells readily supported the proliferation of leukemic cells. Moreover, 5-aza-dC pretreatment partially restored HHIP expression and leukemic cell proliferation on coculture was concomitantly reduced, indicating that aberrant reduction of HHIP expression is associated with the leukemic cell-supporting activity of BM stromal cells.</p><p class="norm">It was previously suggested that genetic abnormalities are necessary for the development of MDS and AML, and genetic aberrations, such as single base changes, deletions, insertions and genomic rearrangements, could be detected in early events during AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS development.<sup><!--bib38--><a href="#bib38">38</a></sup> However, it remains unclear how genetic aberrations could be induced in BM. Mice with conditional knockout of Dicer1 or Sbds in BM stromal cells developed dysplastic changes in hematopoietic cells, subsequent genetic mutations, and eventually leukemic transformation.<sup><!--bib7--><a href="#bib7">7</a>, <!--bib8--><a href="#bib8">8</a></sup> This suggests that stromal dysfunction may be an early event in the development of AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS. Consistent with these findings, the reduction of stromal HHIP expression was observed in AML samples and MDS samples. Thus, reduction may be an early event in AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS patients.</p><p class="norm">The precise molecular mechanism of how stromal HHIP reduction could contribute to the proliferation of leukemic cells remains unclear. In this regard, we and others have demonstrated that Hh ligands, including Ihh and sonic Hh, are highly expressed in CD34<sup><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></sup> stem<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>progenitor cells.<sup><!--bib9--><a href="#bib9">9</a>, <!--bib11--><a href="#bib11">11</a></sup> Moreover, Hh stimulates the cell cycle of hematopoietic stem<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>progenitor cells and erythroid progenitor cells in an autocrine or paracrine manner.<sup><!--bib21--><a href="#bib21">21</a>, <!--bib39--><a href="#bib39">39</a>, <!--bib40--><a href="#bib40">40</a>, <!--bib41--><a href="#bib41">41</a></sup> However, it is unclear how Hh signaling is self-limiting in BM microenvironment.<sup><!--bib15--><a href="#bib15">15</a>, <!--bib16--><a href="#bib16">16</a></sup> In the present study, we found that HHIP is highly expressed in primary stromal cells, but not in primary PB, BM or CB CD34<sup><span class="mb">&#x0002B;</span></sup> hematopoietic cells (<!--fig1--><a href="#fig1">Figure 1d</a>), suggesting that stromal HHIP is part of an inhibitory system of Hh signaling (<!--fig1--><a href="#fig1">Figures 1a and b</a>). Hh signaling has no significant effects on adult hematopoietic stem cell function <i>in vivo</i>.<sup><!--bib12--><a href="#bib12">12</a>, <!--bib42--><a href="#bib42">42</a></sup> Whether the Hh pathway has an important role in malignant hematopoiesis is controversial.<sup><!--bib43--><a href="#bib43">43</a></sup> The expansion of Bcr-Abl-positive leukemic stem cells was dependent on Hh pathway activation<sup><!--bib13--><a href="#bib13">13</a>, <!--bib14--><a href="#bib14">14</a></sup> although Hh signaling is not required for the development of MLL-AF9-mediated AML.<sup><!--bib44--><a href="#bib44">44</a></sup> Collectively, the high level of stromal HHIP expression in BM could suppress the proliferation of Hh signaling-dependent leukemic cells.</p><p class="norm">Understanding the mechanisms of reduced stromal HHIP in leukemia is a critical step towards future therapies. In this regard, we focused on the marked reduction of HHIP expression in several human stromal cell clones and AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS-associated stromal cells (<!--fig1--><a href="#fig1">Figure 1a</a>, Supplementary Figure 1a and <!--fig3--><a href="#fig3">Figure 3c</a>). Analysis of the methylation status of the HHIP promoter region determined that the HHIP promoter was methylated in HHIP-low stromal cells (<!--fig6--><a href="#fig6">Figure 6a</a> and Supplementary Figure 3). These results were consistent with previous reports demonstrating that a 5&#x02032; CpG island existed in the HHIP promoter lesion, and that methylation of the HHIP promoter is detected in some types of tumors.<sup><!--bib25--><a href="#bib25">25</a>, <!--bib45--><a href="#bib45">45</a></sup> Although the demethylating agent 5-aza-dC has begun to be used in the treatment of AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS and genes involved in the regulation of DNA methylation (<i>DNMT3A</i>, <i>IDH1<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>IDH2</i> and <i>TET2</i>) are found to be mutated in MDS,<sup><!--bib46--><a href="#bib46">46</a></sup> the precise molecular mechanism how 5-aza-dC mediates its effects is still unclear especially regarding BM microenvironment.<sup><!--bib47--><a href="#bib47">47</a>, <!--bib48--><a href="#bib48">48</a></sup> Hence, we tested the effects of 5-aza-dC on the leukemic cell-supporting activity of primary AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS-derived cells. Remarkably, HHIP expression in stromal cells was restored following 5-aza-dC treatment and their leukemic cell-supporting activity was concomitantly reduced and associated with demethylation of the HHIP promoter (<!--fig6--><a href="#fig6">Figure 6a</a> and <!--fig7--><a href="#fig7">Figure 7c</a>). Although several transcription factors and adhesion molecules could be associated with the efficacy of 5-aza-dC in the treatment of AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS,<sup><!--bib49--><a href="#bib49">49</a>, <!--bib50--><a href="#bib50">50</a></sup> stromal HHIP may be an important target for 5-aza-dC. Genome-wide analysis, transcriptomes analysis or non coding RNA analysis of AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS-derived stromal cells are required to fully elucidate the mechanisms in future studies.</p><p class="norm">In conclusion, stromal HHIP expression was reduced in AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS-derived stromal cells. The reduction of stromal HHIP expression may correlate with the proliferation of leukemic cells. Thus, aberrant stromal HHIP reduction could contribute to the progression of AML<span class="mb">&#x0002F;</span>MDS.</p></div>
