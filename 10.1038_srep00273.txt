                        <section aria-labelledby=abstract><div class="serif article-section js-article-section cleared clear" id=abstract-section><h2 class="js-section-title section-title position-relative strong tighten-line-height background-gray-light pt20 pb6 pl20 pr20 standard-space-below mq640-pt20 mq640-pb10 mq640-mt0 js-extend-left" id=abstract>Abstract</h2><div class="pl20 mq875-pl0 js-collapsible-section" id=abstract-content itemprop=description><p>Somatic mutation of ten-eleven translocation 2 (<i>TET2</i>) gene is frequently found in human myeloid malignancies. Recent reports showed that loss of <i>Tet2</i> led to pleiotropic hematopoietic abnormalities including increased competitive repopulating capacity of bone marrow (BM) HSCs and myeloid transformation. However, precise impact of <i>Tet2</i> loss on the function of fetal liver (FL) HSCs has not been examined. Here we show that disruption of <i>Tet2</i> results in the expansion of Lin<sup>â</sup>Sca-1<sup>+</sup>c-Kit<sup>+</sup> (LSK) cells in FL. Furthermore, <i>Tet2</i> loss led to enhanced self-renewal and long-term repopulating capacity of FL-HSCs in <i>in vivo</i> serial transplantation assay. Disruption of <i>Tet2</i> in FL also led to altered differentiation of mature blood cells, expansion of common myeloid progenitors and increased resistance for hematopoietic progenitor cells (HPCs) to differentiation stimuli <i>in vitro</i>. These results demonstrate that <i>Tet2</i> plays a critical role in homeostasis of HSCs and HPCs not only in the BM, but also in FL.</p></div></div></section>

                        
                            <section aria-labelledby=introduction><div class="serif article-section js-article-section cleared clear" id=introduction-section><h2 class="js-section-title section-title position-relative strong tighten-line-height background-gray-light pt20 pb6 pl20 pr20 standard-space-below mq640-pt20 mq640-pb10 mq640-mt0 js-extend-left small-space-above" id=introduction>Introduction</h2><div class="pl20 mq875-pl0 js-collapsible-section" id=introduction-content><p>Myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS) and myeloproliferative neoplasm (MPN) are two major hematopoietic malignancies, thought to arise from hematopoietic stem or progenitor cells. MDS is characterized by dysregulated hematopoietic differentiation with propensity to develop acute myeloid leukemia (AML)<sup><a id=ref-link-section-1 title="Corey, S. J. et al. MPN is a spectrum of disease characterized by the expansion of maturing hematopoietic elements with minimal dysplasia. Some of the patients with MPN also progress to myeloid leukemia by acquiring additional mutations altering differentiation. Recent advances in high throughput genome-wide sequencing have made it possible to identify a number of recurrent somatic mutations in human MDS and MPN. One of the most frequently found mutations is a loss-of-function mutation of ten-eleven translocation 2 (<i>TET2</i>), which is located at chromosome 4q24 where uniparental disomy was frequently observed in human myeloid malignancies. Recurrent <i>TET2</i> mutation was identified in 10â20% of MDS and MPN<sup><a id=ref-link-section-2 title="Delhommeau, F. et al. Mutation in TET2 in myeloid cancers. Moreover, it was recently reported that <i>TET2</i> catalytic activity was inhibited by 2-hydroxyglutarate, an abnormal catalytic product generated by mutant <i>IDH1</i> or <i>IDH2</i> proteins that are frequently found in myeloid malignancies. These findings strongly suggest that dysregulation of 5âmC to 5âhmC conversion can be a critical step in myeloid transformation.</p><p>We showed that disruption of <i>Tet2</i> in FL led to increased self-renewal and LTR capacity of FL-HSCs. Furthermore, LSK fraction that mainly consists of MPPs clearly increased in <i>Tet2</i><sup>gt/gt</sup> FL, whereas both percentages and numbers of highly enriched FL-HSC fractions (CD150<sup>+</sup>LSK, CD150<sup>+</sup>CD48<sup>â</sup>LSK and CD34<sup>+</sup>LSK cells) were not significantly different between WT and <i>Tet2</i><sup>gt/gt</sup> mice. These findings are consistent with the ones reported recently for BM cells, showing increased <i>in vitro</i> serial replating capacity and competitive advantage of <i>Tet2</i><sup>â/â</sup> HSCs over WT cells<sup><a id=ref-link-section-32 title="Moran-Crusio, K. et al. Tet2 Loss Leads to Increased Hematopoietic Stem Cell Self-Renewal and Myeloid Transformation. Cancer Cell 20, 11â24 (2011)." href=/articles/srep00273#ref23>23</a>,<a id=ref-link-section-33 title="Quivoron, C. et al. TET2 Inactivation Results in Pleiotropic Hematopoietic Abnormalities in Mouse and Is a Recurrent Event during Human Lymphomagenesis. Cancer Cell 20, 25â38 (2011)." href=/articles/srep00273#ref24>24</a>,<a id=ref-link-section-34 title="Li, Z. et al. Deletion of Tet2 in mice leads to dysregulated hematopoietic stem cells and subsequent development of myeloid malignancies. Blood, in press." href=/articles/srep00273#ref25>25</a></sup>. Taken together with our data, it is suggested that <i>Tet2</i> is critical for HSC/ HPC homeostasis in both FL and adult BM. It should be noted, however, that the previous studies have only examined competitive repopulating capacity of <i>Tet2</i><sup>â/â</sup> HSCs in a single round of transplantation, and did not precisely addressed self-renewal capacity of HSCs by âserialâ transplantation. In contrast, we performed serial transplantation assays and showed that <i>Tet2</i><sup>gt/gt</sup> FL-HSCs presented dramatically increased PB chimerism over WT cells in secondary and tertiary recipients. Furthermore, CD34<sup>â</sup>LSK HSC fraction derived from <i>Tet2</i><sup>gt/gt</sup> FL was significantly increased in the recipientâs BM as compared to the one from WT, indicating that expansion of <i>Tet2</i><sup>gt/gt</sup> HSCs is cell autonomous phenomenon and can occur in the BM microenvironment. Importantly, <i>Tet2</i><sup>gt/gt</sup> cells showed only a mild impairment in myeloid differentiation. Taken together, these data clearly indicate that self-renewal capacity of FL-HSC is enhanced by inactivation of <i>Tet2</i> without major defects on multilineage differentiation capacity.</p><p>Enhanced self-renewal capacity of HSCs by <i>Tet2</i> inactivation is compatible with high incidence of <i>TET2</i> mutation in MDS. MDS is characterized by an expansion of self-renewing malignant clone, which ultimately overrides normal hematopoiesis in the BM. Loss-ofâfunction mutation of <i>TET2</i> clearly endows HSCs with such fundamental feature of MDS, setting a molecular basis for acquiring additional mutations and disease progression. Since <i>Tet2</i> mutation causes only a mild impairment in myeloid differentiation, it seems that clonal evolution of MDS to overt leukemia must include a step acquiring mutation that blocks differentiation.</p><p>We have also shown that CMP fraction, but not GMP and MEP, was significantly increased to 9.04 +/â 1.09% in <i>Tet2</i><sup>gt/gt</sup> FL, as compared to 6.26 +/â 0.53% in WT. Despite some differences in amplifying cell types, CMP amplification is commonly observed either in FL or in the BM. This is in fact consistent with high incidence of TET2 mutation in human myeloid tumors such as chronic myelomonocytic leukemia (CMML), which is characterized by extensive myeloproliferation and myelodysplasia. Recent studies actually reported extramedullary hematopoiesis and the following myeloid transformation in <i>Tet2</i><sup>â/â</sup> mice that was reminiscent of human CMML<sup><a id=ref-link-section-38 title="Moran-Crusio, K. et al. They showed peripheral leukocytosis and splenomegaly with proliferation of myeloid elements occurring in aged mice. Although we could not examine adult <i>Tet2</i><sup>gt/gt</sup> mice due to their perinatal lethality, analysis of <i>Tet2</i><sup>+/gt</sup> mice over 30-weeks of age showed a significant increase of WBC counts in PB. However, in contrast to their results, expansion of HSCs/ HPCs and myeloid cells in spleen could not be documented in <i>Tet2</i><sup>+/gt</sup> mice. Phenotypic discrepancies between the studies also exist in differentiation of myeloid cells in PB. We observed impaired differentiation of <i>Tet2</i><sup>gt/gt</sup> FL cells to Gr-1<sup>+</sup>CD11b<sup>+</sup> mature granulocytes in the transplanted recipients (<a href=#f4>Figure 4</a>), whereas previous studies have shown the increase of mature granulocytes in PB of <i>Tet2</i><sup>â/â</sup> mice<sup><a id=ref-link-section-41 title="Moran-Crusio, K. et al. It is clear that disruption of <i>Tet2</i> critically affects early and late stages of myeloid differentiation, however, revealing the precise molecular mechanism of myeloid regulation by <i>Tet2</i> awaits future investigations.</p><p>Epigenetic modification is a fundamental process for stem cells to maintain pluripotency and capacity to self-renew. Although <i>TET</i> family protein is a major player in this process, the way in which they regulate self-renewal seems different between cell types or among family members. <i>Tet1</i> is essential for self-renewal and maintenance of ES cells (ESCs) as shown by shRNA-mediated knockdown studies, and therefore it has a âpositiveâ regulatory role in this process<sup><a id=ref-link-section-45 title="Ito, S. et al. Therefore, while both <i>Tet1</i> and <i>Tet2</i> are critical for stemness, they work in opposite manner in regulating self-renewal. This may be the reflection of different cellular environment (such as epigenetic status) between ESCs and HSCs, or due to the different inherent function of <i>Tet1</i> and <i>Tet2</i>. Revealing the molecular targets of these genes is absolutely essential for answering these questions.</p><p>In summary, we showed that <i>Tet2</i> inactivation in FL resulted in enhanced LTR and self-renewal capacity of FL-HSCs and altered differentiation in myeloid lineage. However, there still might be unknown features of <i>Tet2</i> that are essential for these processes. Moreover, recent study suggests that the role of TET proteins is not a mere transcriptional de-repressor, but they fine-tunes transcription either positively or negatively, acting as global regulators of transcription<sup><a id=ref-link-section-47 title="Williams, K. et al. TET1 and hydroxymethylcytosine in transcription and DNA methylation fidelity. Nature 473, 343â348." href=/articles/srep00273#ref28>28</a></sup>.
