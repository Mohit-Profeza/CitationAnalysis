                        <section aria-labelledby=abstract><div class="serif article-section js-article-section cleared clear" id=abstract-section><h2 class="js-section-title section-title position-relative strong tighten-line-height background-gray-light pt20 pb6 pl20 pr20 standard-space-below mq640-pt20 mq640-pb10 mq640-mt0 js-extend-left" id=abstract>Abstract</h2><div class="pl20 mq875-pl0 js-collapsible-section" id=abstract-content itemprop=description><p>Deranged metabolism is a hallmark of cancer, playing a significant role in driving the disease process. One such example is the induction of carcinogenesis by the oncometabolite D-2 hydroxyglutarate (D-2HG), which is produced by the mutated enzyme isocitrate dehydrogenase (<i>IDH</i>) occurring in subsets of leukaemias and brain tumours. The oncogenic property of D-2HG appears to stem from its ability to interfere with the activities of Î±-ketoglutarate-dependent dioxygenases, including the Jumonji family histone demethylases. Here, we find in colorectal cancer cells that even in the absence of IDH mutation, the levels of D-2HG and its enantiomer L-2HG were elevated through glutamine anaplerosis. D-2HG, but not L-2HG, increased the trimethylation of histone H3 lysine 4 of the promoter region of <i>ZEB1</i>, a master regulator of epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT), and increased the expression of the <i>ZEB1</i> gene to directly induce EMT in colorectal cancer cells. EMT promotes the ability of cancer cells to invade the local tissue and enter into the bloodstream, leading to distant organ metastasis. D-2HG levels were elevated in colorectal cancer specimens, particularly in those associated with distant metastasis, supporting the observations <i>in vitro</i> and implicating the contribution of D-2HG in metastasis, the major cause of death in this disease.</p></div></div></section>

Dis. 14, 920â930 (2012)." href=/articles/srep36289#ref3>3</a></sup>, underlies the high number of deaths and emphasises the need for novel treatment strategies, underpinned by a better understanding of the disease.</p><p>Deranged metabolism is a hallmark of cancer, including in colorectal cancer<sup><a id=ref-link-section-4 title="Christofk, H. Metabolites are the intermediate products of metabolism, and are the fuel, biomaterials and signalling molecules of the cells<sup><a id=ref-link-section-10 title="Pavlova, N. N. &amp; Thompson, C. B. In two of the three cell lines analysed (DLD1 and RKO), both D- and L-2HG were elevated at levels comparable to or exceeding the concentrations measured in the sarcoma cell line HT1080, harbouring mutant <i>IDH1</i> (<a href=#f1>Fig. 1B</a>). Given that mutations of <i>IDH1</i> and <i>IDH2</i> are the widely known cause of elevated levels of D-2HG, these genes were sequenced, confirming the presence of mutated <i>IDH1</i> (G97D) in the DLD1 cell line, previously revealed to be present in the same cell line<sup><a id=ref-link-section-30 title="Bleeker, F. We also found D-2HG to be elevated in colorectal cancer, particularly in the cases associated with distant metastasis, supporting a significant role of the oncometabolite in the human disease.</p><p>Previous metabolome analyses have shown that the concentrations of a number of metabolites to be deranged in malignancies<sup><a id=ref-link-section-42 title="Kobayashi, T. et al. However, it has been challenging to demonstrate whether the altered metabolite levels are merely the consequence of the disease or whether they have an active role in driving the disease process. In this study, we provide further support that deranged metabolism drives the disease process in cancer, and that this is mediated by metabolites<sup><a id=ref-link-section-44 title="Miyo, M. et al. Here we demonstrate the relevance and the significance of D-2HG in colorectal cancer, showing that endogenous D-2HG levels can be elevated by glutamine anaplerosis in the absence of <i>IDH</i> mutation, and that D-2HG levels are associated with distant metastasis.</p><p>The D-2HG concentrations measured in colorectal cancers were modest relative to cancers that harbour mutant <i>IDH</i>, such as leukaemias and brain tumours<sup><a id=ref-link-section-47 title="Dang, L. et al.
