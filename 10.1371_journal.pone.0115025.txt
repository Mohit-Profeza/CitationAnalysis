  <meta name="description" content="Isocitrate dehydrogenase (IDH), a housekeeping gene, has drawn the attention of cancer experts. Mutation of the catalytic Arg132 residue of human IDH1 (HcIDH) eliminates the enzyme's wild-type isocitrate oxidation activity, but confer the mutant an ability of reducing &alpha;-ketoglutarate (&alpha;-KG) to 2-hydroxyglutarate (2-HG). To examine whether an analogous mutation in IDHs of other eukaryotes could cause similar effects, two yeast mitochondrial IDHs, Saccharomyces cerevisiae NADP+-IDH1 (ScIDH1) and Yarrowia lipolytica NADP+-IDH (YlIDH), were studied. The analogous Arg residues (Arg148 of ScIDH1 and Arg141 of YlIDH) were mutated to His. The Km values of ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H for isocitrate were determined to be 2.4-fold and 2.2-fold higher, respectively, than those of the corresponding wild-type enzymes. The catalytic efficiencies (kcat/Km) of ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H for isocitrate oxidation were drastically reduced by 227-fold and 460-fold, respectively, of those of the wild-type enzymes. As expected, both ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H acquired the neomorphic activity of catalyzing &alpha;-KG to 2-HG, and the generation of 2-HG was confirmed using gas chromatography/time of flight-mass spectrometry (GC/TOF-MS). Kinetic analysis showed that ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H displayed 5.2-fold and 3.3-fold higher affinities, respectively, for &alpha;-KG than the HcIDH R132H mutant. The catalytic efficiencies of ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H for &alpha;-KG were 5.5-fold and 4.5-fold, respectively, of that of the HcIDH R132H mutant. Since the HcIDH Arg132 mutation is associated with the tumorigenesis, this study provides fundamental information for further research on the physiological role of this IDH mutation in vivo using yeast." />

<div class="abstract toc-section"><a id="abstract0" name="abstract0" data-toc="abstract0" class="link-target" title="Abstract"></a><h2>Abstract</h2><a id="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract1.p1" name="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract1.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Isocitrate dehydrogenase (IDH), a housekeeping gene, has drawn the attention of cancer experts. Mutation of the catalytic Arg132 residue of human IDH1 (HcIDH) eliminates the enzyme's wild-type isocitrate oxidation activity, but confer the mutant an ability of reducing α-ketoglutarate (α-KG) to 2-hydroxyglutarate (2-HG). To examine whether an analogous mutation in IDHs of other eukaryotes could cause similar effects, two yeast mitochondrial IDHs, <em>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</em> NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDH1 (ScIDH1) and <em>Yarrowia lipolytica</em> NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDH (YlIDH), were studied. The analogous Arg residues (Arg148 of ScIDH1 and Arg141 of YlIDH) were mutated to His. The <em>K</em><sub>m</sub> values of ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H for isocitrate were determined to be 2.4-fold and 2.2-fold higher, respectively, than those of the corresponding wild-type enzymes. The catalytic efficiencies (<em>k</em><sub>cat</sub>/<em>K</em><sub>m</sub>) of ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H for isocitrate oxidation were drastically reduced by 227-fold and 460-fold, respectively, of those of the wild-type enzymes. As expected, both ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H acquired the neomorphic activity of catalyzing α-KG to 2-HG, and the generation of 2-HG was confirmed using gas chromatography/time of flight-mass spectrometry (GC/TOF-MS). Kinetic analysis showed that ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H displayed 5.2-fold and 3.3-fold higher affinities, respectively, for α-KG than the HcIDH R132H mutant. The catalytic efficiencies of ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H for α-KG were 5.5-fold and 4.5-fold, respectively, of that of the HcIDH R132H mutant. Since the HcIDH Arg132 mutation is associated with the tumorigenesis, this study provides fundamental information for further research on the physiological role of this IDH mutation <em>in vivo</em> using yeast.</p>
</div>

<div id="section1" class="section toc-section"><a id="s1" name="s1" data-toc="s1" class="link-target" title="Introduction"></a><h2>Introduction</h2><a id="article1.body1.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec1.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Isocitrate dehydrogenase (IDH) is an enzyme of the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle and catalyzes the oxidative, NAD(P)<sup>+</sup>-dependent dehydrogenation and decarboxylation of isocitrate to α-ketoglutarate (α-KG) and CO<sub>2</sub>. Due to its critical metabolic function in the cell, IDH is found in organism from all domains of life. IDH can be subdivided according to its cofactor specificity into NAD<sup>+</sup>-and NADP<sup>+</sup>-specific IDHs. Eukaryotes usually have both types of IDH. Eukaryotic NAD<sup>+</sup>-IDH always localizes in the mitochondria and participates in energy metabolism, whereas NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDHs are distributed throughout different cell compartments and function in diverse cellular processes.</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec1.p2" name="article1.body1.sec1.p2" class="link-target"></a><p>The most extensively investigated eukaryotic IDHs are those from human and yeast. Three distinct IDH isoforms are found in human cells. Both cytosolic NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDH (IDH1) and mitochondrial NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDH (IDH2) are homodimers, whereas the mitochondrial NAD<sup>+</sup>-IDH (IDH3) is a heterooctamer <a href="#pone.0115025-Xu1" class="ref-tip">[1]</a>, <a href="#pone.0115025-Soundar1" class="ref-tip">[2]</a>. In addition to their potential catabolic role in the TCA cycle, both IDH1 and IDH2have been demonstrated to aid in the cellular defense against oxidative damage by generating NADPH <a href="#pone.0115025-Shin1" class="ref-tip">[3]</a>–<a href="#pone.0115025-Yang1" class="ref-tip">[5]</a>. In <em>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</em>, there are three homologous NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDHs: mitochondrial NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDH1 (ScIDH1), cytosolic NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDH2 (ScIDH2), and peroxisomal NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDH3 (ScIDH3) <a href="#pone.0115025-McCammon1" class="ref-tip">[6]</a>–<a href="#pone.0115025-Lu2" class="ref-tip">[10]</a>. Each of these proteins plays distinct roles in the central metabolism of <em>S. cerevisiae</em>: ScIDH1 is involved in the cellular glutamate synthesis, ScIDH2 provides NADPH for thiol-based antioxidant systems, and ScIDH3 generates NADPH for the β-oxidation of unsaturated fatty acids <a href="#pone.0115025-Lu2" class="ref-tip">[10]</a>. In <em>Yarrowia lipolytica</em>, a 'non-conventional' species of oleaginous yeast, has only one NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDH (YlIDH), which is located in the mitochondria and is closely related to the oleaginicity of <em>Y.lipolytica</em> <a href="#pone.0115025-Li1" class="ref-tip">[11]</a>.</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec1.p3" name="article1.body1.sec1.p3" class="link-target"></a><p>As a fundamental metabolic enzyme, IDH has been extensively studied both kinetically and structurally. However, few new studies on IDH have been published in recent years. Furthermore, interest in human IDH1 and IDH2 in tumor metabolism studies has recently increased since the publication of a cancer genome project showing that the genes encoding IDH1and IDH2 were mutated in glioblastoma multiforme (GBM), in 2008 <a href="#pone.0115025-Parsons1" class="ref-tip">[12]</a>. The scientific community was further encouraged to consider this abnormality when another cancer genome project also reported the very same mutation in IDH1 and IDH2 of acute myeloid leukemia (AML) patients <a href="#pone.0115025-Mardis1" class="ref-tip">[13]</a>.</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec1.p4" name="article1.body1.sec1.p4" class="link-target"></a><p>Recently, human IDH mutations have been reported to be somatic and monoallelic and to occur at a single residue located in the substrate-binding pocket. Mutations in IDH1 of GBM patient almost always affect arginine 132 (R132) <a href="#pone.0115025-Parsons1" class="ref-tip">[12]</a>, which has been found to be converted to histidine, serine, cysteine, glycine or leucine, with histidine (R132H) being the most frequent change <a href="#pone.0115025-Yan1" class="ref-tip">[14]</a>. GBM-associated IDH2mutations are present at the homologous R172 residue (with the alterations being predominantly R172K), and AML-associated mutations are found at another active site, arginine R140 (with the alterations being predominantly R140Q) <a href="#pone.0115025-Yan1" class="ref-tip">[14]</a>, <a href="#pone.0115025-Ward1" class="ref-tip">[15]</a>. As R132 and R172 are hotspots in the substrate-binding site of IDH1 and IDH2 <a href="#pone.0115025-Xu1" class="ref-tip">[1]</a>, mutations affecting these amino acids lead to structural alteration of the active site of the enzyme, resulting in the simultaneous loss of their normal catalytic activity and the production of α-ketoglutarate (α-KG) and NADPH <a href="#pone.0115025-Zhao1" class="ref-tip">[16]</a>. However, mutant IDH enzymes acquire a neomorphic activity in which the normal product, α-KG, is reduced to 2-hydroxyglutarate (2-HG) while NADPH is oxidized to NADP<sup>+</sup> <a href="#pone.0115025-Ward1" class="ref-tip">[15]</a>, <a href="#pone.0115025-Dang1" class="ref-tip">[17]</a>–<a href="#pone.0115025-Rendina1" class="ref-tip">[19]</a> (<a href="#pone-0115025-g001">Fig. 1</a>). 2-HG is structurally similar to α-KG and acts as α-KG antagonist to inhibit multiple α-KG-dependent enzymes involved in epigenetic regulation, collagen synthesis, and cell signaling <a href="#pone.0115025-Rose1" class="ref-tip">[20]</a>–<a href="#pone.0115025-Lu3" class="ref-tip">[22]</a>. The two most recent studies confirmed that <em>in vivo</em>, IDH2 mutations inhibit differentiation, alter DNA methylation and result in hyper proliferation in diverse tumor contexts. Furthermore, IDH2 is required for tumor maintenance rather than simply for tumor initiation <a href="#pone.0115025-Lu4" class="ref-tip">[23]</a>, <a href="#pone.0115025-Chen1" class="ref-tip">[24]</a>.</p>

In the present study, we studied two yeast NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDHs, <em>S. cerevisiae</em> mitochondrial NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDH1 (ScIDH1) and <em>Y. lipolytica</em> mitochondrial NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDH (YlIDH). Both of the analogous Arg residues of human IDH, Arg132 and Arg148 of ScIDH1 and Arg141 of YlIDH, were mutated to His. Detailed assays were performed to assess the enzymatic characteristics of the wild-type and mutant IDHs. We demonstrated for the first time that alteration of the analogous Arg to His confers NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDHs from the two lower eukaryotes the ability to convert α-KG to 2-HG with moderate catalytic efficiency. Because the HcIDH Arg132 mutation is associated with tumorigenesis, it will be interesting to investigate the physiological role of the mutated IDHs in yeast cells. This study provides fundamental information for further research on the physiological role of this IDH mutation <em>in vivo</em> using yeast.</p>

<div id="section5" class="section toc-section"><a id="s2e" name="s2e" class="link-target" title="Expression and purification of the wild-type and mutant enzymes"></a>
<h3>Expression and purification of the wild-type and mutant enzymes</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>The wild type and mutant forms of ScIDH1, YlIDH and HcIDH were purified from <em>E. coli</em> Rosetta (DE3) clones over-expressing these proteins. The transformed cells were cultured overnight at 37°C in Luria-Bertani (LB) medium containing 30 µg/mL kanamycin and 25 µg/mL chloramphenicol. The cells were then inoculated (1∶100) into 50 mL of fresh LB medium containing the same antibiotics and grown in 250 mL flasks at 225 rpm and 37°C until the culture density reached an OD<sub>600</sub> of 0.5–0.6. Isopropyl-β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) was added to the culture at a final concentration of 0.1 mM with subsequent cultivation for 20 h at 20°C.</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p2" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p2" class="link-target"></a><p>The cells were harvested by centrifugation at 4,000 rpm for 5 min at 4°C and resuspended in equilibration/wash buffer (50 mM sodium phosphate, 300 mM NaCl, pH 8.0). After sonication, the cell debris was removed by centrifugation at 11,000 rpm for 20 min at 4°C. The 6×His-tagged wild-type and the mutant enzymes of YlIDH, ScIDH1 and HcIDH were purified using BD TALON Metal Affinity Resin according to the manufacturer's instructions on ice. The purity of the enzymes was determined by SDS-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE), and the proteins were stained with Coomassie Brilliant Blue R-250.</p>
</div>

<div id="section6" class="section toc-section"><a id="s2f" name="s2f" class="link-target" title="Circular dichroism spectroscopy of the wild-type and mutant enzymes"></a>
<h3>Circular dichroism spectroscopy of the wild-type and mutant enzymes</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec6.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec6.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy was performed using a Jasco model J-810 spectropolarimeter. The ellipticity measurements as a function of the wavelength were performed as described previously <a href="#pone.0115025-Pace1" class="ref-tip">[27]</a>. Purified protein samples (0.2 mg/mL) were prepared in 20 mM sodium phosphate and 75 mM Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> (pH 8.0), and the ellipticity (<em>θ</em>) was obtained by averaging 3 scans of the enzyme solution between 195 and 260 nm at 0.5-nm increments. The mean molar ellipticity, [<em>θ</em>] (deg cm<sup>2</sup> dmole<sup>−1</sup>), was calculated using the relationship [<em>θ</em>] = <em>θ</em>/10nCl, where <em>θ</em> is the measured ellipticity (millidegrees), <em>C</em> is the molar concentration of the protein, <em>l</em> is the cell path length in centimeters (0.1 cm), and <em>n</em> is the number of residues per subunit of enzyme (419 for wild-type and mutant ScIDH1; 422 for wild-type and mutant YlIDH; 420 for wild-type and mutant HcIDH).</p>
</div>

<div id="section7" class="section toc-section"><a id="s2g" name="s2g" class="link-target" title="Enzyme assay"></a>
<h3>Enzyme assay</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec7.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec7.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>The isocitrate oxidation activities of wild-type and mutants of ScIDH1, YlIDH and HcIDH were determined spectrophotometrically by measuring the reduction of NADP<sup>+</sup> to NADPH at 340 nm. The standard assay solution contained 50 mM Tris-HCl buffer (pH 8.0), 2 mM MgCl<sub>2</sub>, 1 mM DL-isocitrate, and 0.5 mM NADP<sup>+</sup> in a 1 mL volume at 25°C. The increase in NADPH was monitored at 340 nm using a thermostated Cary 300 UV-Vis spectrophotometer (VARIAN, USA). The activities were calculated using a molar extinction coefficient of 6220 M<sup>−1</sup>cm<sup>−1</sup>. One unit (U) of enzyme activity was defined as the amount of enzyme that catalyzed the formation of 1 µmol of NADPH per minute under the standard assay conditions. The reaction was initiated by adding the wild-type or mutant enzyme at the appropriate concentrations. To determine the <em>K</em><sub>m</sub> of isocitrate, the concentration of isocitrate was varied, where as the standard concentrations of NADP<sup>+</sup> and Mg<sup>2+</sup> were held constant. The apparent kinetic parameters were obtained by nonlinear regression using the program Prism 5.0 (Prism, GraphPad Software, CA, USA), except that the <em>K</em><sub>m</sub> values of isocitrate of ScIDH1 R148A, ScIDH1 R148E, YlIDH R141A, HcIDH R132H and HcIDH R132A mutants were obtained by linear regression because the weaker affinity for isocitrate.</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec7.p2" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec7.p2" class="link-target"></a><p>The α-KG reduction activities of ScIDH1 R148H, YlIDH R141H and HcIDH R132H were assayed by measuring the conversion of NADPH to NADP<sup>+</sup>. ScIDH1 R148H or YlIDH R141H and or HcIDH R132H was added to 1 mL of an assay solution consisting of 50 mM Tris-HCl (pH 8.0), 2 mM MgCl<sub>2</sub>, 0.2 mM NADPH, and 0.2 mM α-KG, and the decrease of absorbance at 340 nm over time was measured. All kinetic parameters were determined in at least three independent experiments. The protein concentrations were determined using the Bio-Rad protein assay kit (Bio-Rad, USA) with bovine serum albumin as a standard.</p>
</div>

<div id="section8" class="section toc-section"><a id="s2h" name="s2h" class="link-target" title="Gas chromatography/time of flight-mass spectrometer (GC/TOF-MS) analysis"></a>
<h3>Gas chromatography/time of flight-mass spectrometer (GC/TOF-MS) analysis</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec8.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec8.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>Assay solutions consisting of 50 mM Tris-HCl (pH 8.0), 2 mM MgCl<sub>2</sub>, 0.2 mM α-KG, 0.2 mM NADPH and the appropriate amount of purified mutant enzymes (ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H) in a 1 mL volume were incubated in a water bath for 2 hours at 25°C. The reaction mixture was then collected using a Millipore protein concentration kit (USA). The mixture was dried under nitrogen gas, oximated with 40 µL of a 20 mg/mL pyridine methoxyl amine solution at 30°C for 90 min, and then silanizated at 37°C for 30 min using a 40 µL solution of MSTFA and TMCS at a ratio of 99∶1 (vol/vol).</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec2.sec8.p2" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec8.p2" class="link-target"></a><p>The supernatant after derivatization was then analyzed using a GC/TOF-MS system (gas chromatography HP6890, Agilent, USA and time of flight-mass spectrometer, Waters, USA), with a DB-5 chromatographic column (30 m×0.25 mm id, film thickness 0.25 µm, Agilent, USA). The temperature of the column was initially set at 70°C and was then increased by 5°C/min to 300°C. The solvent was delayed for 5 min, a 1 µL sample was injected at a constant velocity at 260°C, and the velocity of the Helium developing solvent was 1 mL/min. The temperature of the ion source was 220°C and the temperature of the union lever was 220°C. The ionizing voltage was 70 eV, and the scanning range of the mass spectrum was between 50 and 800 (m/z).</p>
</div>

<div id="section3" class="section toc-section"><a id="s3" name="s3" data-toc="s3" class="link-target" title="Results and Discussion"></a><h2>Results and Discussion</h2>
<div id="section1" class="section toc-section"><a id="s3a" name="s3a" class="link-target" title="Bioinformatics analysis"></a>
<h3>Bioinformatics analysis</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec1.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>The sequence comparison between the two NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDH from lower eukaryotes, ScIDH1 and YlIDH, and human IDH1 (HcIDH) is presented in <a href="#pone-0115025-g002">Fig. 2</a>. The sequence identity among these three eukaryotic IDHs is greater than 60%, suggesting their comparable positions in evolution. As shown in <a href="#pone-0115025-g002">Fig. 2</a>, Arg132 of HcIDH is an evolutionarily invariant residue in NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDHs. Some other critical residues, such as Tyr139 and Lys212 that are important for isocitrate binding and catalysis in wild-type HcIDH and for α-KG binding and catalysis in the mutant HcIDH are also conserved in ScIDH1 and YlIDH (<a href="#pone-0115025-g002">Fig. 2</a>). The crystal structures of HcIDH and ScIDH1 have been solved <a href="#pone.0115025-Xu1" class="ref-tip">[1]</a>, <a href="#pone.0115025-Peng1" class="ref-tip">[9]</a>, and the secondary structural elements are shown in <a href="#pone-0115025-g002">Fig. 2</a>. The overall three-dimensional structures of HcIDH and ScIDH1 are very similar and consequently share an equivalent isocitrate catalysis mechanism, indicating the possibility that the analogous mutation will also confer to ScIDH1 and YlIDH a similar neomorphic ability as that of HcIDH (i.e., the conversion of α-KG to 2-HG).</p>

<div id="section4" class="section toc-section"><a id="s3d" name="s3d" class="link-target" title="The isocitrate oxidation activities of wild-type and mutated enzymes"></a>
<h3>The isocitrate oxidation activities of wild-type and mutated enzymes</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec4.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec4.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>The HcIDH Arg132 mutation nearly inactivates the enzyme, leading to the loss of the ability to oxidize isocitrate to α-KG. However, the mutation provides a new reductive function of catalyzing the NADPH-dependent converting of α-KG to 2-HG <a href="#pone.0115025-Yan1" class="ref-tip">[14]</a>, <a href="#pone.0115025-Ward1" class="ref-tip">[15]</a>, <a href="#pone.0115025-Dang1" class="ref-tip">[17]</a>, <a href="#pone.0115025-Yang2" class="ref-tip">[18]</a>, <a href="#pone.0115025-Gross1" class="ref-tip">[28]</a>. As the overall structures of yeast NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDHs are highly homologous to those of human IDH1 and IDH2, it can be expected that an analogous mutation introduced into yeast NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDHs will also confer the same neomorphic α-KG reduction ability to the mutated enzyme. We attempted this conversion for two yeast NADP<sup>+</sup>-IDHs, one was the well-known yeast <em>S. cerevisiae</em> (ScIDH1) and the other was the “non-conventional” yeast <em>Y. lipolytica</em> (YlIDH).</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec4.p2" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec4.p2" class="link-target"></a><p>The importance of Arg148, Arg141 and Arg132 to the isocitrate oxidation activity of ScIDH1, YlIDH and HcIDH, respectively, was assessed by site-directed mutagenesis in the first place. Each arginine was mutated into either the small, neutral alanine, which cannot participate in hydrogen bonding, or the large, negatively charged glutamic acid, which will repel the negatively charged isocitrate. The <em>K</em><sub>m</sub> values of ScIDH1 R148A, YlIDH R141A and HcIDH R132A were substantially increased by 216-fold, 31-fold and 270-fold, respectively (<a href="#pone-0115025-t002">Table 2</a>). Meanwhile, their specific activities toward isocitrate were reduced by 3-fold, 22-fold and 135-fold, respectively (<a href="#pone-0115025-t002">Table 2</a>). The mutation of arginine to glutamic acid caused even more severe drop in the wild-type activity, since YlIDH R141E and HcIDH R132E were completely inactivated while ScIDH R148E retained only 3% of the wild-type specific activity (<a href="#pone-0115025-t002">Table 2</a>). Our biochemical data showed that Arg148, Arg141 and Arg132 have pivotal roles in both isocitrate binding and catalysis in ScIDH1, YlIDH and HcIDH, respectively.</p>

These results suggest that there is a large difference in the kinetic properties of wild-type HcIDH as determined by different research groups. For example, the catalytic constant (<em>k</em><sub>cat</sub>) for isocitrate reported by Gross et al. <a href="#pone.0115025-Gross1" class="ref-tip">[28]</a> was more than 10,000-fold larger than that determined by Yang et al. <a href="#pone.0115025-Yang2" class="ref-tip">[18]</a>. Moreover, there are disagreements in the kinetic parameters of the HcIDH Arg132 mutants that were reported by several researchers, as the catalytic efficiency (<em>k</em><sub>cat</sub>/<em>K</em><sub>m</sub>) of the R132H mutant for isocitrate that was reported by Dang et al. <a href="#pone.0115025-Dang1" class="ref-tip">[17]</a> was more than 3935-fold higher than that reported by Yang et al. <a href="#pone.0115025-Yang2" class="ref-tip">[18]</a>. We also introduced the R132H mutation into HcIDH and determined the kinetic parameters of both wild type HcIDH and R132H mutant (<a href="#pone-0115025-t002">Table 2</a>). Our data were in the best agreement with that reported by Yang et al. <a href="#pone.0115025-Yang2" class="ref-tip">[18]</a>, as the catalytic efficiency we determined for HcIDH R132H was in the same order of magnitudes with that from Yang's study (<a href="#pone-0115025-t002">Table 2</a>).</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec4.p5" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec4.p5" class="link-target"></a><p>As shown in <a href="#pone-0115025-t002">Table 2</a>, all five studies confirmed that the impaired activity of HcIDH was caused by a dramatic decrease in the substrate affinity, because the mutants' <em>K</em><sub>m</sub> values for isocitrate were found to be 5-fold to 1500-fold higher than that of the wild-type enzymes. However, the analogous mutations in ScIDH1 and YlIDH did not significantly abrogate the enzymes' binding affinities for isocitrate, as demonstrated by the slightly increased <em>K</em><sub>m</sub> values (approximately 2-fold higher) for isocitrate that were displayed by the ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H mutants compared to those of the wild-type enzymes (<a href="#pone-0115025-t002">Table 2</a>). Despite retain the ability to bind isocitrate, the ScIDH R148H and YlIDH R141H mutants showed a severely reduced rate of converting the bound isocitrate, suggesting that effects of the ScIDH1 and YlIDH Arg mutation occurred via a different mechanism compared to that of HcIDH.</p>
</div>

<div id="section5" class="section toc-section"><a id="s3e" name="s3e" class="link-target" title="The α-KG reduction activities of wild-type and mutant enzymes"></a>
<h3>The α-KG reduction activities of wild-type and mutant enzymes</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec5.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec5.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>The neoenzymatic activity of reducing α-KG to 2-HG was determined using a standard assay. Wild-type ScIDH1 and YlIDH could not catalyze this conversion, as no changes in NADPH were detected in the reaction mix (<a href="#pone-0115025-g005">Fig. 5B</a>). The neoenzymatic activities of ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H were clearly demonstrated by the evident NADPH-dependent consumption of α-KG (<a href="#pone-0115025-g005">Fig. 5B</a>). The catalytic efficiencies of the two mutants showed that ScIDH1 R148H is 1.2-fold more effective than YlIDH R141H at producing 2-HG (<a href="#pone-0115025-t003">Table 3</a>).</p>

The kinetic analysis demonstrated that ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H showed considerable affinity towards both α-KG and NADPH (<a href="#pone-0115025-t003">Table 3</a>). The <em>K</em><sub>m</sub> value of ScIDH1 R148H for α-KG was 32-fold lower than that of the HcIDH R132H mutant determined by Dang et al. <a href="#pone.0115025-Dang1" class="ref-tip">[17]</a>. Similarly, YlIDH R141H had a 20-fold higher affinity for α-KG than HcIDH R132H <a href="#pone.0115025-Dang1" class="ref-tip">[17]</a>. However, the <em>k</em><sub>cat</sub> values of ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H for α-KG were much smaller (approximately 0.04%) than those of the HcIDH R132H and R132C mutants reported from other studies <a href="#pone.0115025-Dang1" class="ref-tip">[17]</a>, <a href="#pone.0115025-Gross1" class="ref-tip">[28]</a> (<a href="#pone-0115025-t003">Table 3</a>). The huge discrepancy in the α-KG reduction activity between the yeast IDH Arg mutants of this study and HcIDH R132H mutant from other groups promoted us to perform the HcIDH Arg mutation experiment in our research as well. The kinetic parameters determined for the HcIDH R132H mutant suggested that the Arg mutation in HcIDH did not confer the mutant enzyme that high activity towards α-KG as reported previously <a href="#pone.0115025-Dang1" class="ref-tip">[17]</a>, <a href="#pone.0115025-Gross1" class="ref-tip">[28]</a>. HcIDH R132H mutant, by contrast, showed lower catalytic efficiency for α-KG when compared to ScIDH1 R148H (18%) and YlIDH R141H (22%) as determined by our assays (<a href="#pone-0115025-t003">Table 3</a>). The specific activities towards α-KG of both yeast IDH Arg mutants and HcIDH R132H mutant were proved to be very similar, being around 0.2 U/mg (<a href="#pone-0115025-t003">Table 3</a>).</p>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec5.p3" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec5.p3" class="link-target"></a><p>The performance of the HcIDH R132H mutant in transforming α-KG to 2-HG was proposed to be assisted by the fine-tuned conformational adaptation of the substrate-binding site <a href="#pone.0115025-Dang1" class="ref-tip">[17]</a>, <a href="#pone.0115025-Yang2" class="ref-tip">[18]</a>. The conservation of amino acid residues at the corresponding positions of ScIDH1 and YlIDH (<a href="#pone-0115025-g002">Fig. 2</a>), and the comparable capabilities of ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H in reducing α-KG demonstrated the similar coordinated conformation-adjusting mechanism in these two mutated yeast IDHs. HcIDH Arg132 mutation is certainly involved in the tumorigenesis, but the detailed roles that HcIDH Arg132 mutants play in cancer cell metabolism has not been clearly demonstrated due to the complexity of human cells. One implication from this study is that the metabolic pathways disturbed by the mutated IDH can be explored using yeast as model organism, because yeast is a much simpler unicellular eukaryote and its molecular interaction networks are highly similar to human cells <a href="#pone.0115025-Pereira1" class="ref-tip">[29]</a>, <a href="#pone.0115025-Guaragnella1" class="ref-tip">[30]</a>.</p>

<div id="section6" class="section toc-section"><a id="s3f" name="s3f" class="link-target" title="GC/TOF-MS identification of 2-HG"></a>
<h3>GC/TOF-MS identification of 2-HG</h3>
<a id="article1.body1.sec3.sec6.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec6.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>High-purity (&gt;98%) 2-HG, ICT and α-KG were identified by GC/TOF-MS as standards, having retention times of 11.94 min (<a href="#pone-0115025-g006">Fig. 6A</a>), 17.44 min (<a href="#pone-0115025-g006">Fig. 6B</a>) and 12.03 min (<a href="#pone-0115025-g006">Fig. 6C</a>), respectively. The corresponding mass spectrogram of the three standards is shown in <a href="#pone-0115025-g006">Figs. 6D-6F</a>. Samples of these action mixtures catalyzed by YlIDH R141H and ScIDH R148H were then characterized in the same manner. 2-HG was identified as the end product for both the YlIDH R141H 11.94 min (<a href="#pone-0115025-g007">Fig. 7A</a>) and ScIDH R148H 11.91 min (<a href="#pone-0115025-g007">Fig. 7D</a>) mutant enzymes in the gas chromatogram, respectively. The mass spectrograms for the YlIDH R141H 11.94 min peak (<a href="#pone-0115025-g007">Fig. 7B</a>) and the ScIDH R 148H 11.91 min peak (<a href="#pone-0115025-g007">Fig. 7E</a>) were both consistent with that of the 2-HG standard (<a href="#pone-0115025-g006">Fig. 6D</a>), conforming the production of 2-HG from α-KG by the two mutant enzymes. Although a conspicuous ICT peak at 17.43 min was both detected in YlIDH R141H (<a href="#pone-0115025-g007">Fig. 7A</a>) and ScIDH R148H (<a href="#pone-0115025-g007">Fig. 7D</a>) in the gas chromatogram, but the corresponding mass spectrograms of YlIDH R141H 17.43 min (<a href="#pone-0115025-g007">Fig. 7C</a>) and ScIDH R148H 17.43 min (<a href="#pone-0115025-g007">Fig. 7F</a>) suggest that the both conspicuous ICT peaks were different from that of the ICT standard (<a href="#pone-0115025-g006">Fig. 6E</a>), demonstrating that ICT was not produced from α-KG by the residual IDH activity of the two mutant enzymes. All substrates were completely transformed into 2-HG in the dependence of NADPH in the assay, as no remaining α-KG was detected in the reaction mixture samples by GC/TOF-MS.</p>

<div id="section4" class="section toc-section"><a id="s4" name="s4" data-toc="s4" class="link-target" title="Conclusions"></a><h2>Conclusions</h2><a id="article1.body1.sec4.p1" name="article1.body1.sec4.p1" class="link-target"></a><p>In summary, we introduced the single point mutations Arg148His and Arg141His into ScIDH1 and YlIDH, respectively, because the analogous mutation was found in HcIDH of tumor patients. As expected, the normal isocitrate oxidation activities of the ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H mutants were nearly eliminated, and the neoenzymatic ability to catalyze the conversion of α-KG to 2-HG was acquired by the mutants, and the catalytic activities of ScIDH1 R148H and YlIDH R141H for α-KG were comparable to that of the HcIDH R132H mutant. Since the HcIDH Arg132 mutation promotes tumorigenesis in human cells, a parallel role of the Arg mutated IDHs in yeast cells deserves in-depth exploration in the future.</p>
